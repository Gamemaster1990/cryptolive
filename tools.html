How the Crypto Profit Calculator Works

The Profit Calculator answers a simple question: "If I bought X coins at price Y and sold at price Z, how much would I make?" It calculates the difference between your entry and exit price, multiplied by your position size, to give you your net profit or loss.

The formula is straightforward: Profit/Loss = (Exit Price − Entry Price) × Quantity. The ROI (return on investment) is calculated as (Profit / Cost Basis) × 100%, showing you the percentage return on your trade independent of position size.

Use this tool to evaluate potential trades, calculate actual performance of completed trades, or compare different exit strategies. For example, if you bought 0.5 BTC at $45,000 and sold at $52,000, your profit would be $3,500 — a 15.56% ROI. The detail grid breaks down your cost basis, final value, profit in dollars, and ROI percentage so you can see the full picture at a glance.

📏 Position Size Calculator

Calculate the optimal position size based on your account balance, risk tolerance, entry price, and stop-loss level. This is one of the most important risk management tools for any trader — it tells you exactly how many units to buy to limit your maximum loss to a predefined percentage of your account.

Position sizing is a risk management technique. Always use stop-losses and never risk more than you can afford to lose on a single trade.

Why Position Sizing Matters

The Position Size Calculator implements the core principle of risk management: determine your position size based on your risk, not your greed. Most traders lose money not because their analysis was wrong, but because they risked too much on a single trade.

The calculation works in three steps:

  1. Risk Amount = Account Balance × Risk % — This is the maximum dollar amount you are willing to lose
  2. Stop Distance = Entry Price − Stop Loss Price — The price difference between your entry and stop loss
  3. Position Size = Risk Amount / Stop Distance — The number of units to buy so that if the stop loss is hit, you lose exactly your risk amount

For example, with a $10,000 account risking 2% ($200), entering at $50 with a stop at $45 ($5 risk per unit), the calculator tells you to buy 40 units (worth $2,000). This ensures your maximum loss is capped at $200 regardless of how far the price might fall below your stop.

DCA vs Lump Sum — Which Strategy Is Better?

Dollar-cost averaging (DCA) is the practice of investing a fixed dollar amount at regular intervals, regardless of price. Lump sum investing means investing the entire amount at once. This calculator lets you compare both strategies using actual 30-day price history.

Historically, lump sum investing tends to outperform DCA in bull markets because markets generally trend upward over time. However, DCA reduces the emotional impact of volatility and can protect against investing right before a market downturn. The "right" strategy depends on your risk tolerance, time horizon, and market conditions.

Try the comparator with different coins and time periods to see how each strategy would have performed. Note that the results are based on historical data and do not guarantee future outcomes.

Understanding Crypto Units

Cryptocurrencies use their own unit systems, which can be confusing for newcomers. The Crypto Unit Converter helps you navigate between denominations seamlessly:

  • Bitcoin: 1 BTC = 100,000,000 satoshis (sats). Satoshis are the smallest unit of Bitcoin, named after its creator Satoshi Nakamoto.
  • Ethereum: 1 ETH = 1,000,000,000 gwei. Gwei (giga-wei) is commonly used for Ethereum gas fees. 1 ETH = 10^18 wei, and 1 gwei = 10^9 wei.

The converter also provides an estimated USD value using current market prices, making it useful for understanding the dollar value of small crypto amounts — like how much 100,000 satoshis is worth, or calculating gas fees in dollar terms.

What Is Impermanent Loss?

Impermanent loss (IL) is a phenomenon unique to automated market makers (AMMs) like Uniswap, PancakeSwap, and SushiSwap. When you provide liquidity to a 50/50 pool, you deposit equal values of two assets. As the relative price of these assets changes, arbitrageurs trade against the pool, and you end up with a different proportion of each asset than you started with.

The loss is called "impermanent" because if prices return to their original ratio, the loss disappears. However, if you withdraw while prices are different, the loss becomes permanent.

Key insights from the calculator:

  • A 2× price change results in approximately 5.7% impermanent loss
  • A 5× price change results in approximately 25.5% impermanent loss
  • Impermanent loss is symmetrical — a 50% drop has the same IL as a 100% gain (2×)

Before providing liquidity, always consider whether the trading fees you earn will outweigh the potential impermanent loss.

Take-Profit Ladder Strategy Explained

A take-profit ladder is an exit strategy where you sell portions of your position at multiple price targets rather than all at once. This approach balances the desire to capture profits with the fear of selling too early and missing further upside.

The calculator helps you plan each rung of the ladder. For each take-profit level, you specify the target price and the percentage of your position to sell. The calculator then shows the profit at each level, the cumulative profit, and the total expected return across all levels.

A typical ladder might look like:

  • TP 1 (+25%): Sell 25% — lock in quick gains
  • TP 2 (+50%): Sell 25% — secure mid-range profits
  • TP 3 (+75%): Sell 20% — take more off the table
  • TP 4 (+100%): Sell 15% — capture the double-up
  • TP 5 (+150%): Sell 15% — ride the remaining runners

The result shows the profit at each level, the cumulative total, average exit price, and total profit percentage. This gives you a clear picture of your planned exit before you even enter the trade.

How Staking Rewards Work

Staking is the process of locking up your cryptocurrency to support the operations of a proof-of-stake (PoS) blockchain. In return, you earn rewards — typically paid in the same token you staked. This calculator helps you estimate your potential staking returns using the compound interest formula.

The formula used is: A = P × (1 + r/n)^(n×t), where:

  • P = Principal (your staked amount)
  • r = Annual percentage yield (APY) as a decimal
  • n = Number of compounding periods per year
  • t = Time in years
  • A = Final amount after t years

The calculator shows both the simple interest version (if you withdrew rewards immediately) and the compound interest version (if you reinvested rewards). The difference between the two illustrates the power of compounding — for example, $5,000 staked at 12% APY for 12 months with daily compounding yields ~$637 in rewards vs. $600 with simple interest.

Crypto Tax Basics

In the United States, the IRS treats cryptocurrency as property for tax purposes. This means every time you sell, trade, or spend cryptocurrency, it is a taxable event — you must report capital gains or losses. Understanding the difference between short-term and long-term capital gains is crucial for tax-efficient crypto investing.

Short-term capital gains (holding period of 1 year or less) are taxed at your ordinary income tax rate, which can be as high as 37% for high earners. Long-term capital gains (holding period of more than 1 year) receive preferential tax treatment with rates of 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on your taxable income.
